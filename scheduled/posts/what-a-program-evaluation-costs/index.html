What a Program Evaluation Costs

Evaluation · Sep 22 · Written by Oscar J Mayorga

Few line items make an executive director hesitate like the one marked "evaluation." You know the work matters. A funder is asking for evidence, a board wants to know whether a program is delivering, and you would rather learn what is true than guess. Yet the quotes come back so far apart that the number stops feeling like a price and starts feeling like a riddle. When one proposal is a fraction of another for what looks like the same study, it is fair to wonder what you are actually buying.

This post demystifies that riddle without pretending there is a sticker price. We explain why quotes vary so widely, the real drivers behind the number, what your budget actually pays for, how to right-size the work to the decision at stake, the questions that make quotes comparable, and when the cheapest option quietly costs the most.

Why do evaluation prices vary so widely?

Evaluation prices vary because "an evaluation" is not one thing. It is a category that stretches from a focused study of a single question to a multi-year, multi-site inquiry involving hundreds of people. Two organizations can use the same word for work that differs by an order of magnitude in effort, and the price follows the effort. A quote is really an estimate of skilled hours: the time to design the study, gather and clean data, analyze it with care, and turn the results into something you can use.

That is why comparing two numbers side by side tells you little until you know what each one covers. A low quote is not automatically a bargain, and a high one is not automatically padded. The useful question is not "why is this so expensive" but "what is included, and does it match the decision I need to make." Price is a function of scope, and scope is a set of choices you can make deliberately rather than absorb by accident.

What drives the cost of a program evaluation?

The number and complexity of your questions set the foundation for everything else. A single, well-bounded question costs less to answer than a sprawling set that reaches into every corner of a program. Each additional question adds design, data, and analysis, and questions that ask "why" or "for whom" demand more than questions that ask "how many." Naming the few decisions that truly hinge on the answer is the single most powerful lever you have over cost.

Method and rigor are the next driver. A study that leans on data you already hold costs less than one requiring an experimental or comparison-group design built to support strong causal claims. Rigor is not free, and it is not always necessary, but when a high-stakes decision rests on the finding, the added rigor is what makes the answer trustworthy (Yarbrough et al. 2011). The right level is the one the decision deserves, no more and no less.

Primary data collection is often the largest variable line. Analyzing records you already hold is comparatively light. Collecting new evidence through surveys, interviews, focus groups, and observations is labor-intensive at every step, from recruiting participants to transcribing and coding what they say. The more voices you gather, and the more care you take to gather them well, the more the work costs, because that care is precisely where much of the value lives.

How do sites, stakeholders, and reporting change the price?

Scale and complexity raise cost in ways that are easy to underestimate. Each additional site multiplies coordination, scheduling, travel, and the effort of making findings comparable across locations. Each additional stakeholder group, whether staff, participants, families, partners, or funders, adds a perspective worth capturing and a round of engagement to capture it. Breadth is not padding. It is often what makes an evaluation credible to the very people who must trust it, but it is real work, and it belongs in the budget on purpose.

Reporting and use support shape the back end of the price. A single written report costs less than a package that adds briefings, facilitated sessions to interpret findings together, and help translating results into action. The difference is not decorative. Evaluation earns its keep only when it is used, and the work of making findings usable is a genuine part of the cost, not an upsell (Patton and Campbell-Patton 2022). Timeline sits alongside all of this. A compressed schedule can raise cost by forcing concurrent effort, while a realistic one lets the work proceed at a sustainable pace.

What are you actually paying for?

You are paying for judgment, not just deliverables. The visible outputs are a report, a presentation, a dashboard. The value beneath them is the expertise to design a study that answers your real question, the discipline to gather and handle data responsibly, and the interpretive skill to say what the evidence means and, just as important, what it does not. A credible evaluator works to professional standards of systematic inquiry, competence, and integrity, and that grounding is part of what your budget secures (American Evaluation Association 2018).

You are also paying for care with the people inside your program. The most useful evaluations treat participants and staff as co-interpreters of what the data mean, not as figures to be counted and set aside. That stance takes time, and it is time well spent, because it produces findings that are both more accurate and more likely to be trusted and acted on. When you weigh a quote, you are weighing not only what you will receive but how the work will be done and whom it will serve.

How do I right-size an evaluation to the decision at stake?

Start from the decision, then scope backward to it. Before requesting a single quote, name the choice the evaluation will inform: whether to renew a program, how to strengthen it, what to tell a funder, where to invest next. A clear decision is a boundary. It tells you which questions belong in the study and which are curiosities you can set aside, and it keeps scope from drifting outward until the budget strains under weight the decision never required.

Right-sizing is not about buying the least. It is about matching rigor to stakes. A low-stakes, formative check-in can be lean and fast. A decision that will redirect significant funding or affect the people furthest from opportunity deserves more rigor, more voices, and more care. A good evaluator helps you find that fit rather than selling you the largest study you will tolerate. Ask candidates to scope to your decision, and treat willingness to right-size as a sign of a partner worth having. If this is your first evaluation, our guide on how to choose a program evaluator walks through the fit questions that matter most.

How do I compare quotes fairly, and when is cheap a false economy?

Comparable quotes require a comparable brief. If you describe the same decision, questions, and constraints to every candidate, their proposals become readable against one another, and the differences reveal genuine choices rather than misunderstandings. Ask each candidate the same set of questions. What will you answer, and how? How much new data will you collect? How many sites and stakeholder groups are included? What will we receive, and how will you help us use it? What is the timeline, and what could change the cost? Consistent questions turn a pile of numbers into a decision you can actually make.

Cheap becomes a false economy when the low price buys work that cannot answer your question. A study underpowered for the decision, a template sold as a custom design, or a report with no support for use can cost more than it saves, because you pay again to redo it, or worse, you act on a finding you should not have trusted. The decision about whether to build the capacity in-house or bring in an outside partner shapes cost too, and our post on internal vs. external evaluators unpacks that trade-off. The goal is not the lowest number. It is the right work at a fair price, sized to the stakes and built to be used.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a program evaluation cost? There is no single price, because scope varies so widely. Cost is driven by the number and complexity of your questions, the methods and rigor involved, how much new data you collect, the number of sites and stakeholder groups, and the depth of reporting and use support. Define the decision the evaluation will inform, then scope to it.

Why are two quotes for the "same" evaluation so different? Because they usually are not the same underneath. One may collect new data across several sites while the other analyzes existing records for one. Ask each candidate the same questions about methods, data collection, scale, and use support so the proposals become genuinely comparable.

How can I lower the cost of an evaluation without undermining it? Narrow to the few questions that truly drive a decision, use data you already hold where it is credible, and match rigor to the stakes rather than defaulting to the largest design. A good evaluator will help you right-size rather than upsell, so the work fits the choice at hand.

Is a cheaper evaluation ever a false economy? Yes. A study that is too thin to answer your question, or a report with no support for putting findings to use, can cost more in the end, because you either redo the work or act on evidence you should not have trusted. Weigh fit and usefulness, not price alone.
